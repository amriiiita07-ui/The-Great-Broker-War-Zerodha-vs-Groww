The Great Broker War:
Zerodha vs. Groww

An exhaustive, data-driven comparative study of India's two most consequential fintech platforms — through a period of explosive growth, regulatory siege, and strategic reinvention.

Executive Abstract In FY2024, Zerodha was the undisputed king of Indian discount broking: ₹8,320–9,994 crore in revenue (sources vary by exact scope), ₹4,700 crore in net profit, a 56% margin, and a brand built without a single rupee of conventional marketing spend. Twelve months later, the kingdom trembled. SEBI's sweeping F&O regulatory overhaul — colloquially the "derivatives winter" — carved 40% out of Zerodha's brokerage revenue by Q1 FY26, exposing the fragility of a business that had bet its engine on weekly-expiry option volumes. Meanwhile, Groww — derided by trading purists as a "mutual fund app with delusions of grandeur" — quietly overtook Zerodha in active users in late 2023, reached 12.6 million actives by June 2025, and completed India's most watched fintech IPO in November 2025 at ₹100 per share, listing at ₹114 and delivering strong post-listing returns. FY25 Groww profits tripled to ₹1,824 crore on revenue of ₹3,902 crore. This paper is the forensic autopsy and forward projection of both platforms — backed by verified financial data, NSE disclosures, and primary sources.
01

Market Context & The Power Shift

The Indian retail investor story is the most dramatic capital markets mobilisation of the 21st century. NSE active clients grew from ~18 million in FY20 to a peak of 49.2 million in FY25 — a 170% expansion in five years, powered by zero-commission trading, smartphone penetration, and the psychological scarring of COVID-era idle savings. Within that tide, two platforms surfed to dominance through opposite philosophies.

The Moment the Throne Changed Hands

Groww overtook Zerodha in NSE active clients in September 2023 — the institutional confirmation came in FY25 data, when Groww's market share hit 26.3% vs Zerodha's 16%, a 1,030 basis point delta. By April 2026, Groww's NSE market share stands at 28% — nearly double Zerodha's 15%. The top 4 discount brokers — Groww, Zerodha, Angel One, and ICICI Securities — collectively account for over 65% of all NSE active clients. This is not a marginal lead; it is a structural realignment of the Indian broking landscape.

🔵 Zerodha — The Precision Machine

Founded 2010 by Nithin Kamath in Bengaluru. Bootstrapped from day one — no VC, no external capital, no dilution. The Kite platform is a masterpiece of trading infrastructure: sub-100ms order execution, a full-featured API ecosystem (Kite Connect), and a product philosophy that treats the retail trader as a professional. Zero marketing spend. Revenue via F&O brokerage (₹20/order), interest on idle client funds, and MTF (launched 2024).

🔴 Groww — The Distribution Engine

Founded 2016 by ex-Flipkart engineers. VC-backed (Peak XV, Tiger Global, Sequoia). Started as an MF distributor, became a stockbroker by 2020. Hyper-growth model: simplified UX, aggressive marketing (~₹300–400 Cr annually at peak), and a "super-app" ambition covering stocks, MFs, lending, insurance, and US equities. IPO'd November 12, 2025 at ₹100/share; listed at ₹114. Q4 FY26 PAT surged 122% YoY.

User & Engagement Deep-Dive

Demographic Topology: Tier 1 vs. Tier 2/3

Zerodha's user base is disproportionately urban, male (est. 75–80%), high-income, and financially literate. Its Varsity educational platform — 15M+ learners — functions as an organic pipeline of serious investors. The Kite API has ~100,000 developer accounts, making Zerodha the backbone of India's retail algorithmic trading ecosystem.

Groww's penetration story is fundamentally different. Per its own RHP disclosures, 83%+ of new users are acquired organically and approximately 81% of users are from outside the top 6 metro cities. Approximately 45% of new Groww users join via mutual fund SIPs, not stock trading. One in every three new SIPs in India is now created on Groww. Users across 98% of India's pin codes use the platform, with a 77.7% three-year retention rate and a DAU/MAU ratio of ~56%.

The data reveals a critical structural asymmetry: Groww has outstanding breadth but thin depth. Its SIP market share by number (18.5%) vastly exceeds its MF AUM share (~2%), signaling that its user base is composed of small-ticket, first-time investors. The ARPU gap is stark — ₹10,500/year for Zerodha's active traders vs. ₹3,100/year for Groww's SIP-dominant base, per India Fintech Substack's April 2026 analysis. This is the "Empty Calorie" problem. However, Groww's customer assets have grown at a 91.09% CAGR from FY23 to FY25 — suggesting cohort maturation is genuinely happening.

Platform Stability & Churn Dynamics

The 2024 bull market peak and the subsequent F&O regulatory shock exposed both platforms' churn vulnerabilities. Between January and August 2025, Zerodha lost approximately 550,000 active clients; its active client base dropped from 7.95 million in February to 7.26 million by August. The overall brokerage industry saw active clients fall from 4.92 crore in FY25 to 4.57 crore in FY26, a decline of 34.88 lakh. Kotak Institutional Equities noted: "The active retail investor base shrank in FY2026, suggesting a gradual weakening in the DIY-investing trend seen over FY2021–25."

Platform outages remain a reputational liability for Groww — multiple incidents during high-volatility sessions (Budget day, RBI policy days) have generated disproportionate social media backlash relative to actual client attrition. Zerodha's Kite platform, built on a proprietary low-latency stack, has maintained a superior uptime record but is not immune.

The Zero-Marketing Moat: Zerodha's Invisible Weapon

Zerodha's Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC) is operationally zero. It acquires customers through: (1) Varsity, its free financial education platform with 15M+ users, (2) organic word-of-mouth from its developer community and API users, (3) earned media from Nithin Kamath's high-credibility public presence, and (4) the Rainmatter ecosystem of fintech startups. Groww's organic acquisition machine is also formidable — 83%+ organic, mostly via referrals and word-of-mouth. But CAC when measured on a loaded basis (brand spend, marketing team, campaigns) runs ₹200–400/user.

Key Data Point: Revenue Per Active User Gap Zerodha generates ₹10,500/year per active user. Groww generates ₹3,100/year. Angel One sits at ₹6,300. This 3.4x ARPU gap is the central tension of the paper: Groww wins volume, Zerodha wins value. The strategic question is which converges first. Groww's ARPU is growing as its customer base matures and adopts cross-sell products. (Source: India Fintech Substack, April 2026)

Product Ecosystem & Technology Strategy

Zerodha's Ecosystem: The Rainmatter Bet

Zerodha's product moat extends far beyond Kite. Rainmatter Capital — its fintech investment arm — has backed over 100 startups in financial services, health, and climate. Portfolio companies include Sensibull (options trading platform), Smallcase (thematic investing), Learnapp, and Sequoia-backed personal finance tools. Varsity, with 15M+ users, functions as an organic lead generation engine for the most financially literate cohort in India.

In May 2025, Groww acquired wealthtech startup Fisdom in an all-cash deal worth $150 million, accelerating its wealth management ambitions.

05

Regulatory Headwinds — The SEBI Factor

The 2024–2025 regulatory cycle was the most consequential event in Indian discount broking history. SEBI's interventions were motivated by a damning self-authored study: 91% of retail F&O traders lost money over FY22–FY24, with aggregate losses exceeding ₹1.8 lakh crore. A follow-up SEBI study in July 2025 found retail traders lost ₹1.05 lakh crore in FY25 alone — an average loss of ₹1.1 lakh per trader. The regulator concluded that the discount broking model — which economically incentivised high-frequency, low-capital derivatives speculation — was systematically destroying household wealth.

Source: SEBI Circulars 2024–2025, Zerodha Blog (Nithin Kamath), Inc42, Z-Connect (April 2026)

Differential Impact: Who Bleeds More?

The answer is unambiguous: Zerodha. F&O contributed approximately 90% of Zerodha's revenues pre-crackdown. Groww's exposure was lower (45–50%) due to its substantial MF and equity delivery base. Nithin Kamath publicly stated the impact would hit "~60% of overall F&O trades" and anticipated a "30–50% revenue drop." The crystallisation: Q1 FY26 saw a 40% YoY collapse in Zerodha's brokerage revenues. Zerodha's FY25 revenues fell 11.2% to ₹8,868 crore with net profit declining 23% to ₹4,237 crore — the firm's first-ever annual decline in its 15-year history.

▶ Fig 5.2 — Zerodha: Revenue Erosion Waterfall FY24 to FY26E (₹ Crore)
Source: Zerodha Blog, Nithin Kamath (Sept 2025), Inc42, Wright Research. Revenue components are estimates based on management commentary.

Groww's brokerage revenue share fell from 85% to 79.49% in Q1 FY26, cushioned by MTF income, personal loans, and fixed deposit distribution. The net profit actually rose 12% YoY in Q1 FY26 to ₹378 crore — a remarkable resilience driven by diversification. This is the structural advantage of Groww's "wide" business model vs. Zerodha's "deep" one.

The True-to-Label Circular: A Secondary Wound

SEBI's "true-to-label" circular on transaction charges — banning exchanges from offering volume-based rebates to brokers — effectively ended the hidden income stream that supplemented Zerodha's (and Angel One's) stated ₹20/trade fees. Nithin Kamath estimated this would cause a 10% standalone revenue dip. This was the first strike of the regulatory barrage, and arrived before the F&O restrictions had even been fully implemented.

Hidden Blind Spots & Asymmetric Risks

🔵 Zerodha — Structural Vulnerabilities
F&O over-concentration (90% revenue pre-SEBI). The regulator didn't just clip the wings; it grounded the plane. FY25 confirmed the first annual revenue decline in 15 years.
Aging brand perception: Zerodha is seen as "the serious trader's app," repelling the next 200 million Indian first-time investors who want simplicity, not sophistication.
No external capital buffer: Zero VC backing means no war chest for product pivots, marketing pushes, or acquisitive growth. Strengths become constraints in a sudden pivot.
Revenue concentration in top 5% of users: ~5% of active traders generate an estimated 60–70% of revenue. This cohort is most likely to migrate to sophisticated alternatives or algorithmic trading systems.
IPO ambivalence: Kamath has publicly resisted listing. This limits liquidity for early employees, constrains ESOP-based retention, and creates succession uncertainty. Nithin Kamath's health scare (cardiac event, 2024) added a dimension of key-man risk that has not been fully resolved.
🔴 Groww — Structural Vulnerabilities
The "Empty Calorie" user problem: 28% market share by active users vs. ~2% MF AUM market share. High user count, low wallet share. Most users invest ₹500–2,000/month.
Post-IPO valuation richness: Listed at an implied P/E of ~34x FY25 earnings. Any earnings miss will be punished by public markets with no forgiveness for a "growth" narrative.
Lending pivot execution risk: Groww Credits (personal loans) is entering a highly competitive NBFC market. NPA risk, regulatory cost, and the operational complexity of lending are structurally different from broking.
Brokerage still 79%+ of revenue: Despite diversification narrative, F&O/broking remains the dominant revenue source. SEBI's next round of restrictions would inflict disproportionate damage.
Post-IPO expectations trap: Public market investors expect consistent growth execution. Any slowdown in active user growth (which fell from 7.24M in Q1 FY25 to 6.12M in Q1 FY26) or margin compression will erode premium multiple rapidly.

The Jio Wildcard: Systemic Threat or Slow Burn?

Jio Financial Services (JFS) has total income of ₹3,274 crore (FY26), Jio Credit AUM grown 2.5x to ₹25,000 crore, and a full regulatory stack (CIC-ND-SI NBFC, JV with BlackRock for AMC). The JFS threat is not imminent in broking — the company has not yet launched a competing discount broker product at scale. But its distribution potential is non-linear: 450 million Jio subscribers represent a customer acquisition machine that would make even Groww's organic flywheel look modest.

The scenario that keeps both Zerodha and Groww's management awake: a JFS-powered zero-commission trading app pre-loaded on every JioPhone, with embedded UPI payments and a BlackRock-branded MF offering. At that point, the CAC for JFS approaches zero for the largest captive audience in Indian financial services history. Neither incumbent has a structural answer to distribution at that scale.

Valuation, IPO Logic & The Three-Year Trajectory

Groww: The Listed Story

Groww (Billionbrains Garage Ventures Ltd) IPO'd on November 12, 2025 at ₹100/share, listing at ₹114 on BSE (+14%) and raising ₹2,985 crore from anchor investors. The implied valuation at IPO was ~₹62,000 crore at approximately 34x FY25 P/E. Q4 FY26 PAT surged 122% YoY, and the stock has risen ~64% from its listing price (per May 2026 data). FY26E revenue guidance is approximately ₹5,000 crore. Q3 FY26 revenue was ₹1,216 crore with PAT of ₹547 crore — annualised run-rate approaching ₹2,000 crore in profit.

The bull case justifies a premium to traditional broking peers on three pillars: (1) superior market share trajectory (28% and growing), (2) cross-sell optionality in lending, insurance, and wealth management, and (3) operating leverage on the fixed-cost tech platform. The bear case argues the stock prices in near-perfection with limited regulatory headroom.

Zerodha: The Hypothetical IPO

Zerodha has consistently declined to list publicly. Kamath's stated rationale: the public markets would impose quarterly earnings myopia on a long-duration capital allocation strategy. Hypothetical valuation framework for a Zerodha IPO: At FY25 confirmed net profit of ₹4,237 crore, applying a sector premium of 25–30x P/E (given brand, zero-CAC model, and Rainmatter optionality), the implied valuation ranges from ₹1.05–1.27 lakh crore (~$12–15 billion). This compares with Groww's IPO valuation of ~$7–8 billion on far lower absolute profitability — suggesting Zerodha is substantially undervalued on a pure earnings basis while Groww commands a growth premium.

The Two Races: Who Wins What?

The Trading Race — Zerodha remains structurally dominant among serious traders. Its Kite platform, API ecosystem, and professional-grade tools create a switching cost that is psychological and technical. Even with F&O volumes structurally lower post-SEBI, Zerodha will retain the high-ARPU trader cohort. The MTF book (₹5,000 crore) and LAS operations provide a credit income layer that partially offsets brokerage losses. Zerodha's ARPU of ₹10,500/year is 3.4x Groww's ₹3,100/year — it wins fewer customers but extracts far more from each.

The Wealth Management Race — Groww is positioned to win this by a material margin. Its ~18.5% SIP market share, AMC launch, Fisdom acquisition ($150M, 2025), and planned expansion into PMS and AIF verticals give it distribution depth that no broker has assembled before. Customer assets grew at a 91.09% CAGR from FY23 to FY25. The ARPU per user is currently low, but the option value of cross-selling a ₹1 crore wealth product to even 0.5% of its 12.6 million active users represents ₹63,000 crore in AUM — a transformative opportunity.

The Final Verdict

This paper has examined two companies that are far more alike than their positioning suggests — and far more different than their financials reveal. Both are under regulatory siege. Both are pivoting from pure brokerage. Both face the Jio wildcard. The question is not which is "better." It is: for which decade?

🔵 Zerodha — The FY2020s Verdict

The greatest capital-efficient business India's startup ecosystem has produced. ₹4,237 crore in confirmed FY25 profit with zero external funding, zero marketing spend, and margins still above 47%. A founding team that understood that the best broker does not want its clients to trade more. The SEBI crackdown is a genuine stress test — but a company with ₹40,000+ crore in estimated net worth and zero debt does not face an existential crisis. It faces a recalibration. The MTF pivot, if executed well, can partially restore the margin structure. Zerodha will survive this decade. Whether it will dominate the next is uncertain — and Nithin Kamath knows it.

🔴 Groww — The FY2030s Bet

The platform that understood that the next 200 million Indian investors were not traders — they were savers who needed a financial GPS, not a racing simulator. One in three new SIPs. 28% NSE market share. A public market listing at ~₹62,000 crore. Q4 FY26 PAT up 122% YoY. The "Empty Calorie" critique is valid but misses the compounding effect: today's ₹500/month SIP investor is tomorrow's ₹5 lakh wealth management client. Customer assets grew at 91% CAGR. The Fisdom acquisition accelerates the wealth management pivot. If Groww cracks cross-sell — and the quarterly numbers suggest it is beginning to — the platform's total addressable revenue could be 10–15x its current base within a decade.

The Single Paragraph Verdict

Zerodha built a monument to capital efficiency in the era of F&O abundance. Groww is building the rails for India's wealth creation decade. The SEBI crackdown did not kill either — it ended the era where being a pure-play derivatives brokerage was a viable long-term strategy. Both platforms now face the same question: can they become financial services companies that happen to have a brokerage license, rather than brokerages that happen to offer financial services? Groww has moved faster and more credibly toward that answer. Zerodha has the deeper moat among traders, the cleaner balance sheet, and the more intellectually honest founding philosophy. In a fair world, Zerodha wins the quality award and Groww wins the market. In Indian fintech, market share has a way of converting into quality. Watch Groww.
